Council Bluffs is a city in and the county seat of Pottawattamie County, Iowa, United States. Located on the east bank of the Missouri River directly across from Omaha, Nebraska, it is a principal city in the Omaha-Council Bluffs metropolitan area. The city had a population of 62,799 at the 2020 census, making it the tenth most populous city in Iowa and the largest in southwest Iowa. The broader metro area had a population of 1,001,010, ranking 51st nationally. The city's coordinates are approximately 41 degrees 14 minutes north latitude and 95 degrees 51 minutes west longitude, at an elevation of 978 feet.

Council Bluffs was incorporated on January 19, 1853. Prior to that date, the area was known as Kanesville, named for Thomas L. Kane, who helped negotiate federal permission for the Mormons to use Indian land along the Missouri River. The name was taken from a bluff about 20 miles to the north where the Lewis and Clark Expedition met members of the Otoe tribe on August 2, 1804. Before European-American settlement, the Iowa side of the river became an Indian reservation in the 1830s for members of the Council of Three Fires, including Chippewa, Ottawa, and Potawatomi peoples, who were forced from the Chicago area under the Treaty of Chicago. Between 1,000 and 2,000 Potawatomi settled in the area, led by their chief Sauganash, also known as Billy Caldwell. The missionary Pierre-Jean De Smet founded St. Joseph's Mission in 1838 to minister to the Potawatomi. Kanesville became the historic starting point of the Mormon Trail and a major outfitting point for westward emigration. By 1846 the Potawatomi were forced to relocate to a reservation in Kansas.

President Abraham Lincoln designated Council Bluffs as the official eastern starting point of the first transcontinental railroad, completed in 1869. The official Mile 0 is at 21st Street and 9th Avenue, now marked by a gold spike monument. The physical rail connection to Omaha across the Missouri River was not completed until the Union Pacific Missouri River Bridge opened in 1872. By the 1930s, Council Bluffs had grown into the country's fifth largest rail center. Grain elevators that supported the city's role as a storage hub still mark the skyline. In 1926, a portion of the city west of the Missouri River seceded to form Carter Lake, Iowa, after the river changed its course.

According to the United States Census Bureau, the city has a total area of 45.672 square miles, of which 42.964 square miles is land and 2.709 square miles is water. The city covers a unique topographic region in the Loess Hills, originally composed of prairie and savanna with extensive wetlands and deciduous forest along the Missouri River. Lake Manawa State Park is located at the city's southern edge.

The 2020 census recorded 62,799 people, 25,271 households, and 15,302 families. Population density was 1,461.7 inhabitants per square mile. There were 27,100 housing units. The racial makeup was 83.7 percent White, 2.7 percent African American, 0.9 percent Native American, 1.0 percent Asian, 0.1 percent Pacific Islander, 4.7 percent from other races, and 6.9 percent from two or more races. Hispanic or Latino residents of any race comprised 10.4 percent of the population, numbering 6,508 people.

At the 2010 census, the population was 62,230 with 24,793 households and 15,528 families. Population density was 1,518.8 per square mile, and there were 26,594 housing units. The racial composition was 90.9 percent White, 1.9 percent African American, and 8.5 percent Hispanic or Latino. Of all households, 31.6 percent had children under 18, 41.4 percent were married couples, and 37.4 percent were non-families. The average household size was 2.43 and the average family size was 3.01. The median age was 35.9 years. The 2000 census reported 58,268 residents with a median household income of 36,221 dollars, median family income of 42,715 dollars, and per capita income of 18,143 dollars. About 8.2 percent of families and 10.3 percent of the population lived below the poverty line.

The casino industry became a significant economic force after Iowa liberalized gambling laws. The Bluffs Run Greyhound Park opened in 1986, followed by riverboat casinos. By 2005, Council Bluffs was the 19th largest casino market in the country with revenue of nearly 434 million dollars. Major casinos include Ameristar, Harrah's, and Horseshoe Council Bluffs. Google built server farms in the city beginning in 2007, with total investment reaching 1.5 billion dollars by 2014, the largest private investment in Iowa history. Industries in the city include frozen foods, robotics, dairy products, plastics, electrical products, and meat packaging, with manufacturers such as ConAgra and Tyson Foods operating plants locally.

Public education is provided by two school districts. The Council Bluffs Community School District operates ten elementary schools, two middle schools, two high schools named Abraham Lincoln and Thomas Jefferson, and several specialized centers. The Lewis Central Community School District also serves part of the city. Private schools include Saint Albert Catholic Schools and several Christian institutions. The Iowa School for the Deaf has operated at the city's south edge since 1870. Iowa Western Community College provides higher education, and Buena Vista University maintains a local campus. Transportation infrastructure includes Interstate 80, Interstate 29, and U.S. Route 6, along with Union Pacific, BNSF, Iowa Interstate, and Canadian National rail lines. The Bob Kerrey Pedestrian Bridge connects walking and cycling trails across the Missouri River to Omaha.
