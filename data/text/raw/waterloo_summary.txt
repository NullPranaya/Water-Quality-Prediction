Waterloo is a city in and the county seat of Black Hawk County in northeastern Iowa, United States. It is the eighth-most populous city in the state, with a population of 67,314 recorded at the 2020 census. Waterloo forms a twin conurbation with the neighboring city of Cedar Falls and serves as the larger of the two municipalities. The Waterloo-Cedar Falls metropolitan area has an estimated population of approximately 170,000 residents. The city is located at coordinates 42 degrees 30 minutes 50 seconds north latitude and 92 degrees 20 minutes 46 seconds west longitude, at an elevation of 883 feet. It was incorporated in 1868 and uses the ZIP codes 50701 through 50707 and area code 319.

The area was originally known as Prairie Rapids Crossing. George and Mary Melrose Hanna and their children became the first settlers on the east bank of the Cedar River in 1845. The name Waterloo was adopted after Charles Mullan, while petitioning for a post office, selected it from a list of existing post office names across the country. The city experienced two major periods of rapid growth: from 1895 to 1915, when the population surged from 8,490 to 33,097 representing a 290 percent increase, and from 1925 to 1960, when it rose from 36,771 to 71,755. The earlier boom was driven by manufacturing, rail transportation, and wholesale operations, earning Waterloo the nickname "The Factory City of Iowa." Key employers during this era included the Waterloo Gasoline Traction Engine Company, the Rath Packing Company, and the Illinois Central Railroad.

According to the United States Census Bureau, Waterloo has a total area of 63.43 square miles, of which 61.59 square miles is land and 1.84 square miles is water. The city is bisected by the Cedar River flowing southeastward. Waterloo has a humid continental climate classified as Koppen type Dfa, and falls within USDA Plant Hardiness zone 5a. The normal monthly mean temperature ranges from 18.5 degrees Fahrenheit in January to 73.6 degrees Fahrenheit in July. Temperature extremes have reached 112 degrees Fahrenheit during the Dust Bowl in July 1936 and negative 34 degrees Fahrenheit in March 1962 and January 2009. Normal annual precipitation is 34.60 inches spread over 112 days, with winter snowfall averaging 35.3 inches across 27 days. The growing season lasts approximately 154 days.

The 2020 census reported 67,314 residents with a population density of 1,092.8 inhabitants per square mile. There were 31,603 housing units at an average density of 513.1 per square mile. The racial makeup was 72.4 percent White, 17.3 percent Black or African American, 2.5 percent Asian, 0.5 percent Pacific Islander, 0.3 percent Native American, and 3.3 percent from other races or two or more races. Hispanic or Latino residents of any race comprised 7.1 percent of the population. From 2000 to 2020, the non-Hispanic White population declined from 80.61 percent to 65.84 percent, while the non-Hispanic Black population grew from 13.77 percent to 17.87 percent, and the Hispanic or Latino population rose from 2.63 percent to 7.12 percent.

At the 2010 census, the population was 68,406 with 28,607 households and 17,233 families. Of these households, 29.9 percent had children under 18. Married couples made up 40.3 percent of households, and 39.8 percent were non-families. The average household size was 2.35 and the average family size was 2.95. The median age was 35.9 years, with 23.7 percent under 18 and 14 percent aged 65 or older. The gender split was 48.4 percent male and 51.6 percent female.

Waterloo's economy is centered on agricultural manufacturing, healthcare, meatpacking, education, banking, and tourism. John Deere, which acquired the Waterloo Gasoline Engine Company in 1918, remains a major employer with approximately 4,300 employees, though layoffs in 2024 and 2025 further reduced its workforce. The city suffered significantly during the agricultural recession of the 1980s, when John Deere cut 10,000 jobs and the Rath meatpacking plant closed with 2,500 job losses, resulting in an estimated 14 percent population decline. Other major employers include UnityPoint Health with 3,805 employees, Tyson Foods with 2,969, MercyOne with 2,392, and the Waterloo Community Schools with 1,750. The city has two hospitals: MercyOne Waterloo Medical Center with 366 beds and UnityPoint Health Allen Memorial Hospital with 234 beds.

Waterloo operates under a mayor and council system of government, with five ward-based council members and two at-large seats. The current mayor, Dave Boesen, took office on January 1, 2026. Almost all of the city falls within the Waterloo Community School District, which operates three public high schools: Waterloo West, Waterloo East, and Expo. Private high schools include Waterloo Christian School and Columbus Catholic High School. Hawkeye Community College is located in Waterloo, while the University of Northern Iowa is in neighboring Cedar Falls. Transportation infrastructure includes Interstate 380, U.S. Highways 20, 63, and 218, the Waterloo Regional Airport with American Airlines service to Chicago, and a metropolitan bus system serving Waterloo and Cedar Falls. Cultural attractions include the Waterloo Center for the Arts, the Grout Museum District, the Lost Island Theme Park, and the Cedar Valley Arboretum spanning 40 acres.
