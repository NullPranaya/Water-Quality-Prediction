Des Moines is the capital and most populous city in the U.S. state of Iowa. It serves as the county seat of Polk County, with parts extending into Warren County. The city is named after the Des Moines River, derived from the French Rivière des Moines, meaning River of the Monks. Des Moines was incorporated in 1851 as Fort Des Moines and shortened to its current name in 1857. According to the 2020 Census, the city had a population of 214,133, making it the largest city in Iowa by both population and land area.The six-county Des Moines metropolitan area encompasses Polk, Dallas, Warren, Madison, Guthrie, and Jasper counties. As of 2024, the metro population was estimated at 779,048, ranking it the 78th largest in the United States and the largest located entirely within Iowa.Geographically, Des Moines is in the south-central part of Iowa at the confluence of the Des Moines and Raccoon Rivers. According to the United States Census Bureau, the city covers a total area of 90.65 square miles, of which 88.93 square miles is land and 1.73 square miles is water. The city sits at an elevation of approximately 850 feet above sea level. In 2009, the city annexed 5,174 acres and gained approximately 868 new residents, with an additional 759 acres voluntarily annexed during the same period.
Des Moines experiences a humid continental climate classified as Köppen Dfa. Summer temperatures frequently reach the 90s and occasionally exceed 100 degrees Fahrenheit. Average annual snowfall is 36.5 inches, and annual precipitation averages 36.55 inches, concentrated in warmer months. The city experienced significant flooding during the Great Flood of 1993, when the Des Moines Water Works was submerged, leaving approximately 250,000 people without running water for 12 days and without drinking water for 20 days. Major flooding occurred again in June 2008.
The 2020 census recorded 87,958 households and 48,599 families, with a population density of 2,428.4 persons per square mile. There were 95,082 housing units at an average density of 1,078.3 per square mile. The average household size was 2.5, and the average family size was 3.3. The median age of residents was 34.8 years.
Demographically, the racial composition of Des Moines in 2020 was 64.54 percent white, 11.68 percent Black or African American, 6.76 percent Asian, 0.69 percent Native American, 0.06 percent Pacific Islander, 6.62 percent other races, and 9.65 percent two or more races. Hispanic or Latino residents of any race comprised 15.64 percent of the population. The non-Hispanic white population stood at 60.99 percent. Des Moines has the largest African American population in Iowa.
The 2016-2020 American Community Survey estimated the median household income at 54,843 dollars and the median family income at 66,420 dollars. Males had a median income of 38,326 dollars compared to 29,855 dollars for females. Approximately 12.1 percent of families and 16.0 percent of the total population lived below the poverty line, including 24.3 percent of those under 18 and 9.8 percent of those 65 and older. Among the population aged 25 and over, 86.7 percent held a high school diploma or higher, and 27.9 percent held a bachelor's degree or higher.
Des Moines is a major center for the insurance industry and financial services. The city headquarters the Principal Financial Group and Wellmark Blue Cross Blue Shield. Other major corporations with operations in or near the metro include Wells Fargo, Cognizant, Voya Financial, Nationwide, Bayer, and Corteva. Technology companies Microsoft and Hewlett-Packard have built data-processing facilities in the area. Iowa's low insurance premium tax rate of one percent contributes to the city's appeal as an insurance hub.
The city operates under a council-manager form of government. The council consists of a mayor elected citywide, two at-large members, and four ward representatives. A 2004 proposal to merge the governments of Des Moines and Polk County was rejected by voters. The Des Moines Public Schools district is the largest in Iowa, with over 32,000 enrolled students across 63 schools including 38 elementary schools, eleven middle schools, and five high schools. Higher education institutions include Drake University, Grand View University, Mercy College of Health Sciences, and Des Moines University, an osteopathic medical school. Des Moines Area Community College serves as the regional community college.
Des Moines holds political significance as the site of the first caucuses in the presidential primary cycle. The city maintains 76 parks covering over 3,000 acres and 45 miles of trails. Cultural attractions include the Des Moines Art Center, the Science Center of Iowa, the Pappajohn Sculpture Park, and the Iowa State Capitol with its 275-foot gold-leafed dome. The annual Iowa State Fair draws over one million visitors. Des Moines International Airport provides nonstop domestic air service, and public transit is operated by DART, consisting entirely of bus routes including express and commuter service to surrounding suburban areas.
